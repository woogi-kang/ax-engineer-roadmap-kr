# 1. Outcomes and Boundaries

## Purpose

Define the workflow outcome to change, potential harm, accountable owner, and stop conditions before choosing tools or implementation.

## Core questions

- Who struggles, and in what situation?
- What evidence describes the current state?
- Is an AI-enabled approach likely to improve it?
- Who could be harmed, and how, if it fails?
- Which decisions and actions must remain with people?
- What outcome would cause the implementation to stop?

## Key activities

1. Distinguish users, process owners, and people affected by the workflow.
2. Collect baselines for current time, cost, errors, waiting, and rework.
3. Separate goals into efficiency, quality, risk, and business outcomes.
4. Define scope and non-goals.
5. Set the initial ceiling for AI autonomy and prohibited actions.
6. Set review dates for scale, improvement, maintenance, or stop decisions.

## Deliverables

- One-sentence problem statement
- Outcome contract
- Baseline
- Risk tier and hard boundaries
- Scope and non-goals
- Stop conditions

## Exit criteria

- The intended outcome can be explained without naming a model or tool.
- Success and failure are expressed as observable states.
- Workflow outcomes and AI quality metrics are separate.
- The final decision-maker and accountable owner are known.
- Prohibited AI actions are documented.

## Stop signals

- No clear user experiences the problem.
- There is no practical way to observe the baseline.
- A harmful action cannot be reversed.
- Automation is requested without an accountable owner.
- The solution is predetermined and counterexamples cannot be considered.

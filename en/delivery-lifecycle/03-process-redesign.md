# 3. Process Redesign

## Purpose

Before adding AI to the current process, redesign what should be removed, combined, standardized, or kept as human judgment.

## Classification

| Class | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Remove | A step that does not contribute to the outcome |
| Combine | Steps that repeat the same information or decision |
| Standardize | A step whose input, output, and criteria should share a contract |
| AI assist | AI supports search, summary, or drafting while a person decides |
| Approval-based execution | The system executes an AI proposal after human approval |
| Bounded automatic execution | Execution within controlled risk and recovery boundaries |
| Human judgment | Contextual, accountable, or ethical judgment remains with a person |

## Key activities

1. Identify the reason and user for each current step.
2. Apply removal, combination, and standardization first.
3. Narrow the input, decision, and output of each AI-enabled step.
4. Design normal, exception, and manual fallback flows together.
5. Record how roles and approval points change.
6. Define the parallel-operation period and retirement conditions for the old process.

## Deliverables

- Current-to-target flow comparison
- Target workflow
- Human and AI responsibility table
- Exception and manual fallback paths
- Old-process retirement plan

## Exit criteria

- The old process has not simply been automated unchanged.
- Every AI-enabled step has a clear user and outcome.
- The reason and criteria for human approval are documented.
- The workflow can fall back to a workable manual path.
- Role changes and old-process retirement conditions are included.

## Stop signals

- The new flow creates more steps and owners than the old one.
- Human approval exists only as a way to avoid accountability.
- The workflow cannot continue at all when automation fails.
- Time saved in execution is exceeded by review and correction time.

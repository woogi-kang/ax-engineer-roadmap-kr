# 7. Adoption and Role Change

## Purpose

Connect the deployed capability to the official workflow and change users, responsibilities, procedures, training, and support.

## Key activities

1. Have real users perform representative and exception flows.
2. Observe trust, correction burden, accessibility, and conflicts with the old process.
3. Operate training, support, and incident-inquiry channels.
4. Confirm the process owner, operator, data owner, and approver.
5. Define the parallel-operation period for old and new procedures.
6. Decide retirement, retention, and recovery conditions for the old procedure.
7. Agree where saved time and changed responsibilities will be reallocated.

## Adoption perspectives

| Measure | Question |
|---|---|
| Access | Can the people who need it actually use it? |
| Repeat use | Do they choose it again for the same workflow? |
| Correction burden | Does time spent correcting results decrease? |
| Completion | Does the new flow reach the workflow outcome? |
| Trust | Can users understand evidence and failure states well enough to decide? |
| Workflow impact | Do time, quality, risk, or business outcomes change? |

## Deliverables

- User acceptance record
- Training and support materials
- Responsibility matrix
- Adoption and workflow-outcome review
- Decision to retire or retain the old process

## Exit criteria

- An operator other than the implementer can perform the core flow.
- Reasons for non-use are separated into capability, trust, training, or process.
- The AX Engineer does not personally handle every routine operating request.
- The old process's state and final owner are explicit.
- Adoption metrics and workflow-outcome metrics are separate.

## Stop signals

- Lack of training is interpreted as resistance.
- Only logins and generated-item counts are reported as impact.
- The official procedure stays unchanged while the new tool is merely recommended.
- Developers leave before operational handoff.
